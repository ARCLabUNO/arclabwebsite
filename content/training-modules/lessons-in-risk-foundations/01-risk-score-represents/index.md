  <div class="arc-module-kicker">Module 1 · Risk Scores</div>
  <h2>What a risk score represents</h2>
  <p>
    A risk score is often treated like a label—low, moderate, or high. But that is not
    really what the score means. A risk score does not tell you exactly what will happen
    to one person. It tells you what tends to happen among people with similar scores.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="arc-module-thesis">
  <strong>Key takeaway</strong>
  <p>Risk scores are group-level probabilities, not exact predictions.</p>
</div>

<div class="arc-module-section">
  <h2>The Common Misunderstanding</h2>
  <p>
    Risk scores are often treated as fixed categories. But they are better understood as
    positions along a continuum.
  </p>
  <div class="arc-module-key">
    <strong>The idea:</strong> A risk score is not a label—it is a position within a distribution.
  </div>
</div>

<div class="arc-module-section">
  <h2>How Risk Scores Usually Look</h2>
  <p>
    Scores tend to form a distribution that looks like a bell curve. Most people cluster
    in the middle, with fewer at the extremes.
  </p>
</div>

{{< risk-score-built-figure >}}

<div class="arc-module-section">
  <h2>How to Read the Figure</h2>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Lower scores</strong> → lower outcome rates (~10%)</li>
    <li><strong>Middle scores</strong> → moderate rates (~25%)</li>
    <li><strong>Higher scores</strong> → higher rates (~50%)</li>
  </ul>
  <p>
    The score does not predict an individual outcome. It tells you what tends to happen among people with similar scores.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="arc-module-bottom">
  <h2>Bottom Line</h2>
  <p>
    Risk scores are group-level probabilities—not exact predictions.
  </p>
</div>

</div>
