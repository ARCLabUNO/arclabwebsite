<div class="arc-module-hero">
  <div class="arc-module-kicker">Module 7 · Theory &amp; Dynamics</div>
  <h2>Support is not always protective.</h2>
  <p>
    One of the most important criminogenic needs is social support. But support is not
    always protective. When people hear “social support,” they often think of something
    positive. Yet relationships can influence behavior in very different ways.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="arc-module-thesis">
  <strong>Key takeaway</strong>
  <p>The people closest to us help shape what becomes normal, acceptable, and expected.</p>
</div>

<div class="arc-module-section">
  <h2>Supportive Relationships Can Reduce Risk</h2>

  <p>
    Supportive relationships can provide:
  </p>

  <ul>
    <li>accountability</li>
    <li>encouragement</li>
    <li>emotional support</li>
    <li>stability</li>
    <li>access to prosocial opportunities</li>
  </ul>

  <div class="arc-module-key">
    <strong>The idea:</strong> The question is not simply whether someone has support. The question is what kind of support they are receiving.
  </div>
</div>

{{< social-support-figure >}}

<div class="arc-module-section">
  <h2>Relationships Can Also Reinforce Risk</h2>

  <p>
    Social relationships can also reinforce risk.
  </p>

  <p>
    Examples include:
  </p>

  <ul>
    <li>criminal associates</li>
    <li>antisocial peers</li>
    <li>enabling intimate partners</li>
    <li>substance-using social networks</li>
  </ul>

  <p>
    These relationships can normalize offending, provide criminal opportunities, and
    reinforce antisocial behavior.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="arc-module-section">
  <h2>What Kind of Support?</h2>

  <p>
    Historically, many assessments treated family relationships and peer relationships
    as separate domains.
  </p>

  <p>
    More recent perspectives increasingly recognize that both may reflect a broader
    concept: social support and social bonds.
  </p>

  <p>
    The important question is often not who the relationship is with, but whether the
    relationship promotes stability and prosocial behavior or reinforces risk.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="arc-module-section">
  <h2>Why This Matters</h2>

  <ul>
    <li>Social relationships can increase or decrease risk</li>
    <li>The quality of support matters more than the mere presence of support</li>
    <li>Building prosocial support networks is often a major intervention goal</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<div class="arc-module-bottom">
  <h2>Bottom Line</h2>
  <p>
    The people closest to us help shape what becomes normal, acceptable, and expected.
    Social support matters because it can either promote stability or reinforce risk.
  </p>
</div>
